# From Junior to Senior: The Non-Technical Path

Most engineers think seniority is about knowing more technologies. It's not. It's about judgment, communication, and the ability to make others around you better.

## Learn to Say "I Don't Know"

Juniors bluff. Seniors admit ignorance and then go find the answer. Credibility compounds faster when people trust your honesty over your bravado.

## Ask Before Building

Before writing a single line of code, ask: What problem are we solving? Who has this problem? How will we know it's solved? Building the wrong thing fast is worse than building the right thing slowly.

## Code Reviews Are Teaching Moments

A senior engineer's code reviews don't just catch bugs — they educate. Explain the "why" behind your suggestions. "Use a HashMap here" teaches nothing. "A HashMap gives O(1) lookups vs O(n) for this list iteration" teaches everything.

## Communicate Tradeoffs

Every decision has tradeoffs. Seniors present options with honest assessments, not just their preferred solution. "We could use Redis for caching. It's fast but adds operational complexity. Memcached is simpler but less feature-rich."

## Own Your Mistakes

When something breaks, the first words out of your mouth should not be defensive. "I made a mistake. Here's what happened. Here's how I'll prevent it next time." This builds more trust than any technical feat.

## Mentor Relentlessly

Your impact multiplies when you help others grow. Pair program, review code patiently, answer questions thoroughly. A senior engineer who hoards knowledge is just a senior in title.

---

The path from junior to senior is not a ladder of technical skills. It's a widening circle of responsibility, empathy, and impact.
